The Crypto King's Muse

by Francesca Frost

Charlotte Gordon Lennox doesn't have time to mourn her losses. Once a high-flying fashion entrepreneur, she's now buried in debt, hustling for survival in the volatile world of cryptocurrency. When a job at Riverbank—a powerful crypto platform—promises financial salvation, she grabs it. But there's a catch.

One of Riverbank's enigmatic cofounders is Duane Blacklock, a brilliant, secretive CEO known throughout the industry as the Crypto King—and whose path once collided with Charlotte's in a way she's never forgotten or forgiven.

Now, working side by side, their chemistry is undeniable—but so are the shadows they both carry. As Charlotte rises inside Riverbank, she's drawn deeper into Duane's gravity: his brilliance, his obsession, his ghosts from a violent past. And as their worlds collide, she becomes the muse—and the weakness—of a king who will risk everything to keep her.

From Manhattan to the Cayman Islands to the glittering chaos of Davos—where the line between control and surrender blurs, and love becomes the most volatile trade of all.

The Florentine Lion

by Francesca Frost

Florence has a way of interrupting your thoughts, at first gently, elegantly, then all at once. I went there for a writing retreat with romantic yearnings to be infused with art and beauty, a notebook filling itself, the Arno stirring bold emotions into lyrical passages. But what I really found was a kind of quiet confrontation. One afternoon wandering in the Palazzo Medici Riccardi to escape the bright, insistent Tuscan sun, I turned a corner and met a lion.

He wasn’t roaring. He wasn’t mid-pounce. He sat in stone patience beneath an archway, massive paws planted, mane carved into disciplined curls. The air was cool and faintly dusty. The palazzo felt like old money and older secrets. And walking back and forth, I watched this magnificent creature, recognizing something in him. His strength without performance, dominance without noise.

Later I learned Florence’s lion has a name: the Marzocco. He is the city’s symbol, the emblem of Florentine identity, pride, and civic power. The name traces back to Marte, Mars, the Roman god of war—Florence’s earlier emblem—so even when the image is “only” a lion, the lineage speaks of power, conquest, defense, survival. The Marzocco is not a mascot. In Florentine history he was a warning and a promise, depending on which side of the gates you stood.

Months afterward when I needed an emblem for my imprint, for Frost House, my mind went back to that lion in the palazzo. I didn’t want a delicate flourish or something merely pretty. I wanted an imprint with backbone. And because my stories move between seduction and threat, tenderness and dominance, I wanted a visual paradox. Something that held both.

In the first designs, he had a frosted mane, white powder like snow. The heat and desire of romance set against frost. Stoic eyes, not sentimental ones. Not a lion “in love,” but a lion who understands love’s cost. But design is also a compromise, especially when you want an image that can be reproduced cleanly, stamped small, printed in black and white, and still look like itself.

The lion became a simpler emblem, more easily repeated and connected to the imprint as a door knocker: Frost House. The frost wasn’t only in the mane anyway, it was in the mood, the edge, the discipline. The cold is what makes the heat feel dangerous.

I’m aware that romance novels are still often dismissed as soft, feminine, light, a guilty pleasure, a fluffy escape. But that has never matched my experience of love, on the page or in life. It is a negotiation. It is a strategy, a surrender, power dynamics, and vulnerability, the risk of being seen, chosen, and greatest of all, needed. Love can be playful, yes, and it can be absurdly funny. But it is also ferocious. It is warlike in its stakes: what you win, what you lose, what you become. That’s what I write.

So the Frost House lion is my quiet rebuttal, sitting there like the Marzocco did, still, watchful, unafraid. A promise that what looks “romantic” can also be ruthless and what looks “cold” can conceal the most dangerous kind of heat.

“I’m not going to be gentle. I know what you need, know what you want. This is you and me.”

Francesca Frost
